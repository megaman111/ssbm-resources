This is a collection of all the information I think is really important if you want to take a deep dive into a comprehensive approach to teams. Teams is a complicated but really fun mode with a lot of nuances that at first seem random but can be made less random with good awareness and coordination.

It should also be noted that a good singles player will only amplify their skill as a doubles player. Simply being able to hold your own in singles neutral and then also having these concepts in your pocket will help you excel with many types of teammates.

Key Differences: Singles vs Doubles - NMW

Neutral Game

  • Singles: There is no way to be a remotely good singles player without an excellent understanding of neutral game - it's possibly the most important and probably the most difficult part
  • Doubles: If you are playing a support role in doubles, it's possible to win playing no neutral game whatsoever
  • Singles: About a dance to jockey for advantageous positioning - you play patient and find your spot, then take your punish as far as it goes
  • Doubles: About trying to create situations where your opponents have no options whatsoever

Escape Options

  • Singles: Besides wobbling and a few chaingrabs, you can pretty much always do something to escape situations
  • Doubles: There are countless situations where there is absolutely nothing you can do
  • If you are playing against a team that isn't on point about capitalizing on overextensions to create their own "100-0" situation, it's worth playing risky and aggressive
  • Most teams are not on point about turning reversals into no-win situations

Situational Understanding

  • Singles: Situational understanding is probably the most important thing, but there is little consensus on this
  • Doubles: Situational understanding is by far and away, unequivocally the most important thing to playing doubles
  • Singles: There are objectively and obviously best situations
  • Doubles: Has a lot of situations where you have alternative options that are all good, and it's not clear what to do - learn these ahead of time

Risk/Reward

  • Because you have a partner to back you up, the risk/reward of certain options is completely different from singles
  • Because the logic of which options are good in singles doesn't necessarily make sense in the context of doubles, you really need to figure out things ahead of time
  • Coordination is paramount, so you and your partner need to be on the same page about which options you favor in which situations

Improvisation vs Flowcharts

  • Doubles: Much more about improvisation and much more free flow than singles
  • Singles: Lets you get away with camping into flowchart punishes if you are good at staying safe and have good execution
  • Simply because there are innumerable situations with 4 players on the screen compared to 2, doubles forces countless novel situations which you need to learn to navigate

Time Management

  • Time is the most important resource in doubles
  • Earning time to look at the entire screen in order to assess where to position yourself is a victory in itself
  • Learn which situations allow you to take advantage of the time you've earned
  • Don't squander advantages you've gained by choosing options that take too much time
  • Waiting is bad - doubles favors being proactive far more than singles
  • If you don't feel the urgency when you are playing, odds are that you have poor situational awareness
  • There's almost always useful things to be done on the screen

Screen Awareness

  • How to watch the screen while watching or playing doubles is a skill to be learned
  • You need to develop the ability to track all four players simultaneously
  • This is fundamentally different from singles where you primarily focus on your opponent

Doubles for Singles Tryhards - NMW

In a community largely composed of motivated young individuals who strive to climb to the peak of the mountain, investing time in doubles often seems like a diversion. Being a good doubles player involves a different skillset, but honing your doubles skills can result in improvements in singles as well. If you struggle with any of the following things, playing doubles will accelerate your growth as a singles player, probably even more than playing singles.

Fighting in Close Quarters / Microspacing

  • Teams, especially on Yoshi's, involves a shitton of hitboxes delicately spaced around both your opponent and teammate
  • If you are antsy when people enter the edge of your range, this will quickly vanish from playing some good sessions of teams
  • Your "bubble" is constantly violated, and you need to learn your hitboxes, their hitboxes, and how to drift/pivot/etc.
  • If you watched G3 GF, HMW+Phil talk about how Armada vs. Leffen matches tend to be played at mid and long range, but Mango vs. Armada was all being played at close range
  • If you suck at close range, play teams

Making Decisions Quickly

  • Situations arise much more quickly in teams - opportunities are fleeting
  • In singles, you often camp in neutral, find your spot, then do your rehearsed punishes
  • Teams has way more situations and infinitely more improv
  • You will learn to recognize situations and to react quickly by practicing teams

Being Slippery and Not Getting Locked Down

  • Whenever your teammate dies, you need to be slippery
  • Moves have frame commitments, but movement doesn't necessarily
  • Watching good doubles players like PewPewU, you can see how slippery they manage to be to avoid getting crushed in 2v1 situations
  • If you are one of those people who consistently throws out moves at the same ranges instead of opting to maneuver around situations, playing teams, and especially 1v2, will immensely help you

Seeing Opportunities for Crossups

  • Watch how PewPewU creates offense with crossups in 2v2
  • Watch how he does it in 1v2 situations
  • In the same vein as how you will learn to maneuver better, you will learn to smell the right time for crossups

Getting Out of the Corner

  • It's very rare for you to get lucky and get out of the corner in teams
  • Rolling in generally doesn't work
  • Dashing aerials are likely to get punished by the back line
  • You have to get real good to reclaim stage position unless you can ledgedash like Hax

Getting Your Moves Out on the Earliest Possible Frames

  • As mentioned before, singles has a lot of neutral
  • Teams has a lot of "heads up" situations where you try to throw out your move to beat your opponent's
  • Getting your moves out as quickly as possible is super important
  • You will learn to react to whiffs and hits on shield, as well as hit confirms because you need to get subsequent moves out quickly as well

Dealing with Crouch Cancel

  • Crouch cancel is annoying - grabs tend to beat it, but sometimes grabs are not a good option
  • Learning how to space your moves and move quickly out of them to avoid getting CC punished is huge in teams
  • If you play a team without a spacie, CC can wreck your day if you aren't cognizant of it
  • You'll get really good at anticipating and playing around CC playing one of these compositions

Ledge Tech Skill

  • Melee is a game played at the ledge
  • Control of the ledge is way more important in teams than in singles
  • More of teams is going to be played at the ledge
  • You will be forced to ledgedash, refresh invincibility, and so on to reclaim stage position

Getting Out of Shield

  • Shielding is better in teams because there are way more situations where grabs aren't threatening
  • Not only does this result in you being in shield more, but you have to figure out how to get out in more challenging situations
  • You can have your roll cutoff while you are getting shield pressured, or you can have two people sandwiching your shield with hitboxes like spacie uptilts, staggering their shieldstun
  • Shield way too much in singles? At least you'll learn to escape quickly from teams practice

Shield Angling / Shield DI / Light Shielding

  • As mentioned, you'll be in shield a lot more
  • Using these defensive techniques goes from being a nice helpful bonus to something essential as fuck

Mashing

  • You gots to get outta grabz or else u gonna get fukt
  • Teams will force you to develop your mashing game

Reading Techs

  • Tech chasing with regrabs or things that slowly rack up damage is not a good idea in teams
  • Going for a hard punish based off a tech read is better the vast majority of the time
  • It's also easier to read techs in teams because more options are cut off
  • Think of it like training wheels - you'll do it a lot more, and it'll be a bit easier, so then you'll be primed to do it when necessary in singles

Anticipating Recovery Options / Ledgeguarding from the Stage

  • Closing out ledgeguards as quickly as possible is important - protracted ledgeguards are bad
  • You want to do a 1-2 step ledgeguard, not 5+ steps
  • Therefore, reading and going for the coup de grace is better
  • In spite of this, going offstage is often risky, so you'll also learn to force people onstage and to ledgeguard them there
  • Note: People tend to do this too much rather than not enough, but it's still a valuable skill

Recovering with Randall

  • People go for Randall way more because the ledge is always covered
  • Learn the timer, learn to use him, learn to stop people from using him

Extending Recoveries to Get More Opportunities

  • Ledge teching 10 times, v-canceling, etc. are pointless a lot of the time in singles because they just reset you to a ledgeguard that anyone decent will hit
  • If you Falcon kick after getting hit off at 100, you can keep living until 170, but you don't gain any height most of the time
  • Still, getting more chances is never a bad thing - in doubles, it's a SUPER good thing
  • You'll get better at living longer, and depending on which ledgeguard options your opponent opts for, you might even get to turn it around on them off a ledge tech

L-Canceling Against Ice Climbers

  • Hitting multiple targets, especially when you are unsure of how many you will hit, only happens in doubles and against ICs
  • How this helps should be obvious

Knowing When to Get Nana (The Answer: You Probably Shouldn't as Much as Commentators Say)

  • Target selection is another thing that's only relevant against ICs
  • "Always go for Nana" is bad advice - go for Popo when it's a guaranteed kill
  • It's easy as fuck to knee ICs out of their side+b if it's only Popo
  • Popo's recovery without Nana is seriously Falcon caliber
  • In teams, you are often put in situations where you need to decide whether to ledgeguard or give it up and do something across the stage
  • Like when facing ICs, it's usually better to go for the guaranteed thing across the stage (team save, team combo is like killing Nana) but sometimes you go for the kill on your side
  • It'll make it easier for you to decide when not to go for Nana, which is the default option

Quick Start Guide - RunRiot

This is a very surface level guide to doubles. This is a list of things you should start focusing on first so that it isn't an overwhelming task. Once you start to understand the basics you can begin to look into the guides below for some more in-depth explanations. Keep in mind there will always be exceptions, but this is a nice easy explanation of things to start with if you are new to the format.

This is a very general list. These are not strict rules but rather a starting point.

The Do's

  • Stay close to your teammate but don't overlap with them
  • Rely on your teammate to help you defensively
  • React for team combos
  • Use projectiles or throws to stop bad situations
  • Be smart about positioning and preventing worst case scenario

The Don'ts

  • Don't cross over or stand on your teammate, and don't go for things they are already accomplishing
  • Don't use projectiles in the direction of your teammate (unless it is to assist in a disadvantageous position)
  • Don't hold or push someone to ledge when your teammate needs it
  • Don't throw away a stock to try to save someone who has a very slim chance of coming back (remember, each team has 8 stocks they share)
  • Don't try to use moves to defend yourself or use moves as panic options
  • Don't overextend because you don't trust a teammate to do something

Doubles Neutral Game - RunRiot

Understanding Neutral in Doubles

  • The doubles neutral game is similar to singles - it's about adjusting positioning to force an opening on the opponents
  • The fight for positioning while keeping risk/reward in mind for both you and your teammate is what makes up doubles neutral
  • The tools you use to achieve solid positioning will vary by character and team composition, but should always be something you look to improve and understand
  • Teams awareness goes a long way towards decision making for achieving good position
  • Think of your neutral game as a combined goal for you and your teammate - your neutral isn't separate
  • Your goal and gameplan should ideally function as a unit with your teammate

Center Stage Control - RunRiot

Why Center Stage Matters

  • Controlling center stage is the first thing that can truly give you a huge advantage as a teams player
  • Center stage allows you to box the other team out
  • You can reach your teammate quicker from center
  • Center allows you to abuse space more effectively
  • Increases survivability as a team
  • Makes doubles a much more structured format than you might be accustomed to
  • Every single good team will be thinking about how to take center and be very cautious of leaving that space
  • Most important: Controlling center gives you a CHOICE in the gamestate

Spacing with Your Teammate - RunRiot

The "Roll Distance" Rule

  • Keep about a roll distance away from your teammate as a general rule of thumb
  • This spacing depends on how much space the character needs, but it's a good baseline
  • This is often referred to as maintaining your "side"

Key Rules for Teammate Spacing

  • Almost NEVER cross over your teammate - there are exceptions, but in general let your teammate keep their position
  • This reduces confusion because it keeps your position structured and productive
  • Close enough to assist your teammate but far enough to give them the space they need
  • If you stay too close, your teammate can't comfortably interact with the situation
  • If you are too far away, you cannot assist them or damage control if they start being punished

Advanced Spacing for Sandwich Combos - NMW

  • The optimal spacing for sandwiching is roughly one Pokemon Stadium Pokeball width apart
  • This distance allows both players to hit the sandwiched opponent without hitting each other
  • At this spacing, you can use short hop aerials that drift toward the opponent
  • Spacies can shine after aerials at this distance, making it especially effective
  • The sandwiched opponent has very limited escape options at this spacing
  • Adjust spacing based on the opponent's percent - closer at low percent, farther apart at high percent
  • Be aware of moves with large hitboxes that might hit your teammate

Maximizing Damage in Sandwich Situations - NMW

  • When sandwiching, alternate attacks to keep constant pressure
  • Use moves that knock the opponent toward your teammate, not away
  • Short hop aerials are ideal because you can drift and adjust spacing mid-attack
  • Don't both attack at the same time - this wastes hitstun and can cause you both to get punished
  • The goal is to build percent quickly until you reach knockdown percent
  • Once you have knockdown, you can start true team combos
  • Fighting opponents at 0% is difficult due to crouch cancel, so sandwiching to build percent is crucial

Platform Usage - RunRiot

General Platform Strategy

  • Platforms allow you to stay safer when facing a lot of danger, such as spawn invincibility or an unfavorable 2v1
  • Platforms become important when facing characters that are slower or can't challenge them as well

Top Platform Control

  • The top platform is much like the top of the key - on top platform you can truly control the gamestate
  • You can access every position on the stage in a reasonable amount of time
  • Can stop high percent team combos as they usually occupy the height of the top platform

Side Platforms

  • Side platforms are smaller versions of top platform control
  • They serve a similar purpose but with more limited coverage

Damage Control - RunRiot

Understanding Damage Control

  • Damage control is positioning that allows you to disrupt a beneficial situation the opponents might have
  • Keep in mind which moves you use and the timings - some moves are much better and lower risk
  • Make sure you do not put yourself in danger when attempting damage control
  • Be very mindful when trying to control a negative situation as it can often become much worse if not a good decision

Best Moves for Damage Control

  • Pikachu's up air
  • Fox shine/drill
  • Peach up-B
  • Projectiles are the most prominent damage control tools - they disrupt with usually no repercussion for using them
  • The negative of projectiles is they can be blocked
  • Projectiles are the best at controlling a situation to give advantage to your team

Damage Control Warnings

  • Badly placed projectiles are very dangerous as they can completely stop an advantage your teammate might have
  • Some of the best damage control by the ledge is just not getting involved
  • Don't become a part of the damage if it isn't worth the risk

Three Categories of Team Compositions

  • 2 Slower/Stationary Characters
  • 2 Space Taking Characters
  • 1 of Each

General Strategy by Character Type

  • In general, you want to abuse the weaknesses of each type of character
  • Sandwiching every composition is not always a good idea
  • Creating space at the cost of a punish can be a very good choice

Space Taking Characters

  • Example: Captain Falcon is a space taking character
  • Preventing him from being productive by sandwiching him is very useful

Slower/Stationary Characters

  • Example: A character like Samus who enjoys controlling interactions
  • Becomes very non-threatening when thrown away

Example: Fox/Sheik vs Fox/Fox at Low Percent

  • Throwing a Fox away to 2v1 the other can buy enough time to create an opening
  • This is better than trying to sandwich two low percent fastfallers and getting reversaled
  • You will start to understand the exceptions when you keep these principles in mind

Target Switching - RunRiot

Understanding Target Switching

  • Target switching is something almost exclusive to doubles and may take the longest to truly master
  • It's the interaction by one person to win neutral against two people by abusing the unpredictability of being able to threaten both of them
  • Target switching is a habit you can pick up on - keep in mind if someone is target switching to the same tempo

As the Single Person

  • Awareness to see the whole screen is key
  • You need to be able to understand when you can pick your spots
  • Be able to track both opponents at the same time (although you should probably do this anyway)

Starting a Punish - RunRiot

Time Considerations

  • In singles the combo goes as long as you can make it, but in doubles it is the exact opposite (barring a 2v1)
  • The faster and more efficient the punish is, the harder it is for the opponent's teammate to stop it in time
  • There are many more things you need to consider to make sure the punish is very solid in doubles

Teammate Availability

  • You must consider if your teammate is available to help
  • If your teammate is in a bad situation, you might give up your punish altogether
  • If your teammate is doing a combo themselves, then you both have the freedom to do individual combos
  • If your teammate is with you and boxing out the opponent, or the other opponent can't reach, then your teammate can help you

Value of Knockdowns

  • Knockdowns are incredibly useful in doubles
  • The knocked down opponent cannot help or interact for that amount of time

Punish Columns - RunRiot

Understanding Punish Columns

  • Punish columns describe positioning during a team combo
  • A team combo generally has the victim go back and forth between teammates while rising in height
  • This relates back to letting hitstun breathe - you have more time than you think

Spacing by Percent

  • Start close to your teammate at lower percent to pass them back and forth
  • Get farther apart as the percent gets higher
  • No two teammates should hardly ever be in the same column
  • Horizontal combos are generally much safer and easier to execute than vertical ones

Tech Chase Columns

  • Punish columns also exist for tech chases
  • You can discuss with your teammate who takes the in place options

React in Team Combos

  • Please react in team combos - you have more time than you think
  • Don't rush or panic, let the hitstun breathe

Knockback Stacking - RunRiot & NMW

Understanding Knockback Stacking

  • This topic is important to recognize during a team combo
  • Knockback stacking is necessary to understand for team combos and getting pick-ups on punishes

How Knockback Stacking Works

  • Within 10 frames: Moves that hit within 10 frames of hitstun starting will send with the knockback of the latest move hit
  • Applications are mostly for saving (using a weak move to save from Puff's rest) or combo extension with the weak move's hitbox
  • 11 frames and onwards: Moves that hit 11 frames and onwards after hitstun starting will stack the vectors of the moves
  • This lets you do stacking shenanigans to extend combos or hit harder than you normally would if you plan for the stacking

Knockback Canceling - NMW

  • When two moves hit within 10 frames of each other, the second move's knockback completely replaces the first
  • This can be used to save teammates from powerful moves by hitting them with a weak move
  • Example: If your teammate gets hit by a strong move that would kill them, you can hit them with a weak move within 10 frames to cancel the knockback and save them
  • This is especially useful for saving from Jigglypuff's rest or other high-knockback moves
  • The key is timing - you must hit within that 10-frame window for the knockback to be replaced

Advanced Knockback Stacking Applications - NMW

  • After 10 frames, knockback vectors add together rather than replace each other
  • You can use this to extend combos by hitting at angles that complement each other
  • Example: A horizontal hit followed by an upward hit can create a diagonal trajectory that's harder to DI
  • Stacking can also be used to kill earlier by combining multiple strong hits
  • Understanding the timing and angles is crucial for maximizing damage in team combos

Team Tech Chasing - RunRiot

The Challenge of Team Tech Chasing

  • This is the most difficult part of teams punish without discussing it with your teammate beforehand
  • With no prior communication, it's very difficult to know who will take the in-place options without overlap

Ground Tech Chase Coverage

  • Ideally, one person will take each away option and whichever person you decide will take the middle
  • You can decide you will be the "tech chaser" for a couple of reasons:
  • Character: Speed and grab range give more leniency
  • Reaction time: Who has faster or more consistent reactions

Platform Tech Chasing

  • Platform tech chasing is similar to ground tech chasing
  • The person who takes the middle option will be whoever the opponent is closest to on either side of the platform

Top Platform Tech Chasing

  • For the top platform, if possible should be done the same way as side platforms
  • Sometimes compositions only have one character who can make it there in time
  • In that case, that character should do an individual tech chase

Grabs in Doubles - RunRiot & NMW

Why Grabs Are Essential

  • Grabs are just as essential if not more so in doubles
  • Shielding is a really good defensive option in doubles - it allows stalling for time while holding a position
  • Light shield can make it difficult to get shield pokes or push them to a more favorable spot
  • Shielding allows their teammate to help them by hitting their shield to force you to give up the punish or shield
  • Because of this, grabs become much more important to keep opponents from gaining these advantages

Grab Applications

  • Many situations where grabs lead to kill confirms or nuanced situations like grab release
  • Generally grab is the single best way to prevent someone from keeping that position
  • There are no options defensively that beat grab other than avoiding it (not including using a hitbox)
  • If you want to force someone away from ledge, grab forces them to give up that position by avoiding it or being grabbed and thrown away
  • Grabs are generally the safest option in a 2v1 to guarantee a kill confirm

Hold Times and Kill Confirms

  • Hold times are relatively long, especially at higher percents
  • There are a lot of true kill confirms from holding someone
  • Most character's grabs tend to be low risk and low commitment

Grab Release - NMW

  • Grab release is a good situation to keep in mind
  • If a teammate is holding someone while facing the ledge, hitting them with a move causes a grab release
  • The person who was grabbed is sent away at a downward angle
  • This is especially good against fastfallers, as they fall further down and have a hard time recovering
  • This can be done with any move but is especially good with low percent moves and projectiles

Forced Grab Releases - NMW

  • You can force a grab release by hitting the grabbed opponent with specific moves
  • The grabbed opponent will be sent in the direction they were facing when grabbed, at a downward angle
  • This is particularly effective near the ledge for edgeguarding setups
  • Weak moves and projectiles are ideal for forced grab releases as they don't risk breaking the grab too early
  • Timing is crucial - hit too early and you might save them, hit at the right time and you set up a devastating edgeguard
  • Characters with fast, weak moves (Fox jab, Falco laser, Sheik needles) excel at forced grab releases
  • This technique is especially powerful in 2v1 situations where you can guarantee the setup

Grab Hold Strategies - NMW

  • Holding an opponent in grab creates a temporary 2v1 situation
  • The longer you hold, the more time your teammate has to position for a follow-up
  • At higher percents, hold times increase significantly, giving more setup time
  • Use pummels strategically - they add damage but reduce hold time
  • Communicate with your teammate about when you'll throw or release
  • Holding near the ledge while your teammate charges a smash attack is a guaranteed kill setup
  • Be aware of the opponent's teammate position - they may try to interrupt

Throws in Doubles - RunRiot

Understanding Throw Options

  • Throws are a unique part of the punish game
  • There are so many variations and choices for throws that it's important to understand how you can use your character's throws to achieve the goal

Passing Throws

  • If the goal is to pass to your teammate, make sure to grab in the direction for the better passing throw if given the option
  • Example: At higher percent you want to pass with Fox backthrow instead of forward throw to avoid the deadweight, making it easier for the teammate to pass them back

Low Percent Throw Strategy

  • At really low percents, you might want to do a quick move after your throw to build percent so they don't fall out of the combo
  • Example: You might consider upthrow upsmash with Fox first before passing so that a fastfaller would not fall to the ground too fast before trying to pass at zero percent

Knockdown Throws

  • Throws that cause a knockdown are very good
  • Tech chasing is a lot easier with two people

Throwing Offstage

  • Throwing offstage can be a very good option if the edgeguard is executed correctly

Throw Invincibility

  • For all throws, as soon as the throw starts you gain 8 frames of invulnerability
  • This especially comes in handy when holding someone "hostage" and the opponent trying to help their teammate is predictable
  • This is useful when you get a grab knowing you might be punished - you can use the throw invulnerability to protect yourself
  • The person you grabbed still has a chance to be hit by that move during your invulnerability
  • Faster grabs are much better due to every grab giving 8 frames of invulnerability
  • The closer the grab is to 8 frames long, the higher ratio of invulnerability the throw will have

Grab States - RunRiot

Understanding Grab States

  • One of the more subtle parts of teams is understanding grab states
  • There are two states of when someone is grabbed in Melee:
  • Air grab: When the character grabbed does not have their feet touching the ground
  • Ground grab: When the character has at least one foot touching the ground

Why Grab States Matter

  • Certain moves act differently to an opponent depending whether they are in the air or on the ground
  • Example: Fox's up tilt on Captain Falcon (grounded) will knock him out of the grab and over Fox's head
  • Example: Fox's up tilt on Jigglypuff (aerial) does not cause the grab to break because they are in the air during the grab
  • This is because Fox's up tilt has more base knockback on a grounded opponent compared to an aerial opponent

Spikes and Meteors

  • Spikes/Meteors will knock a grounded opponent up
  • An aerial opponent will get knocked down into a tech chase

What Determines Grab Break

  • Percent is NOT a factor for if someone will break out of a grab from a hit
  • ONLY the move staling and the base knockback of the move itself determine this
  • Some characters will always end up in a grounded state when grabbed
  • Other characters are dependent on who they are grabbed by
  • The grabber height, the grabbee height, and the grab itself all play a role in what happens

Height for Combos - RunRiot

Understanding Combo Height

  • Height for combos is a very overlooked part of doubles combos

Team Edgeguarding - RunRiot

Three Types of Team Edgeguards

  • One person onstage, one person on ledge
  • One person going offstage, one person on ledge
  • Both players onstage, but one covering ledge

One Person Onstage, One Person on Ledge

  • Most traditional edgeguard and the most consistent
  • Holding ledge and forcing someone onstage is invaluable due to most characters having a period of vulnerability when landing onstage
  • Characters that take time offstage are much more vulnerable to characters on the ledge
  • Allows the team to cover every option with a ledge roll and a hitbox onstage
  • Allows the easiest time preventing the opponent's teammate from coming to assist
  • The person onstage can wall the teammate while the person on the ledge takes over the edgeguard

One Person Going Offstage, One Person on Ledge

  • A little riskier but is usually better for quicker kills
  • Prime example: A shine spike attempt - one of the fastest kill confirms offstage but requires a commitment
  • Ideally the person on the ledge comes up from ledge and protects the person who went offstage
  • Most likely the best option when taking a stock is very crucial to the state of the game

Both Players Onstage, One Covering Ledge

  • Very rarely better than the other two, but there are advantages
  • Most likely the least risky option but is also the easiest to avoid
  • This option is the easiest to set up in a short amount of time
  • Example: Peach is slow but can make up for it by having one of the best ledge covering moves into a strong move
  • This position allows for quick readjustments if needed
  • Better for characters with poor ledge options

Percent-Based Edgeguard Strategy

  • How low the percent of the person being edgeguarded is can affect what method you use to edgeguard them
  • Because time is so valuable in doubles, if given the option you should choose to get an edgeguard as quickly as possible with the resources your team has
  • Generally this is accomplished by having a member go offstage and secure the kill quickly
  • Another option: Stall the person with a repeatable edgeguard to build damage while your teammate protects you

The Importance of Grabbing Ledge

  • Grabbing the ledge is invaluable in an edgeguard
  • This removes not only their ability to stay safe, but some characters will use ledge invincibility to force you to give up a position you may want to keep
  • If you are ever in doubt regarding time or options, just hold ledge and reduce the chance of them finding a safe option
  • This is especially good for covering high recoveries as it prevents them from getting a drift back
  • Which character grabs ledge can be situational, but if given the choice you should have someone predetermined to grab ledge if there is an option
  • If there is not enough time, any character grabbing ledge is fine too

Preventing Teammate Interference

  • This is the process of preventing the teammate of someone being edgeguarded from salvaging the stock or creating a reversal
  • Obviously there's preventing the person onstage from physically reaching their teammate - this is generally a neutral situation in your favor since they are forced to approach
  • More complex is preventing help from projectiles
  • The awareness to block or eat projectiles is very important when securing an edgeguard
  • Light shielding is one of the best ways to block projectiles - the shield is physically much bigger and can block more projectiles
  • If you are light shielding and predicting them to jump over to avoid the shield, you can stuff the jump with an aerial or put yourself in the path
  • Eating the projectile is usually very worth the trade - for example, eating a full set of needles in exchange for a stock is a very good trade

Stalling Opponents

  • This is useful when there is a beneficial situation that you want your teammate to be able to keep
  • Being able to stall to keep them from helping is very effective and can generally turn into a great situation
  • Chaingrabbing, moves that send upwards, and grabs with pummels paired with a throw away from the situation can all be very good methods of keeping someone preoccupied
  • There are also characters that can stall someone offstage, such as Fox drilling from ledge over and over to keep them from coming back but preventing them from dying

Advanced Ledgeguarding Tactics - NMW

  • Ledge hogging timing: Practice grabbing ledge at the exact moment to deny recovery - too early and they can wait you out, too late and they grab it first
  • Ledge trump setups: If your teammate is holding ledge, position yourself to cover all on-stage options with a single move
  • Invincibility cycling: Use ledge invincibility to safely cover recovery options, then let your teammate take over
  • Stage spike setups: Position near the wall to cover tech attempts after stage spikes
  • Double ledge coverage: One player on ledge, one player covering high recovery - this covers all options
  • Projectile edgeguarding: Use projectiles to force recovery timing while your teammate covers the ledge
  • Ledge roll punishes: The on-stage player should always be ready to punish ledge rolls with a charged smash attack

Invincibility Management - NMW

  • Respawn invincibility: When you respawn, you have significant invincibility - use it aggressively to create openings
  • Double invincibility timing: If both players die close together, stagger your respawn drops so invincibility doesn't overlap
  • Ledge invincibility: Use ledge invincibility to safely interrupt edgeguards on your teammate
  • Invincible ledgedash: Fox's invincible ledgedash is one of the few times you should attack from ledge in doubles
  • Respawn platform timing: Wait on the platform if your teammate is about to die - double invincibility is extremely powerful
  • Aggressive invincibility usage: Don't waste invincibility by playing passively - use it to take stage control
  • Invincibility baiting: Opponents must respect your invincibility - use this to force them into bad positions
  • Covering teammate with invincibility: If your teammate is in a bad spot, use your respawn invincibility to save them

Improving at Teams: Taking Time into Consideration - darkatma

Time vs Singles Considerations

  • One of the major differences between teams and singles is the consideration of time
  • In singles, we mainly consider damage output and positional advantage when making decisions during a combo
  • In teams, these two points have to be considered in relation to time
  • A Falco can spend ten seconds pillaring and racking up damage in singles, but the longer a combo extends in teams, the more likely the opponent's teammate will hop in and interrupt
  • Sheik can downthrow techchase over and over in singles, but downthrow takes up a whole second and is easily interruptible in teams

Character Viability in Teams

  • When we re-examine each character's options in relation to time, we can see why certain characters are better or worse in teams compared to singles
  • Worse in teams: Falco's solo combos take too long to rack up damage, Marth's standard edgeguards take too long, and Samus often takes too long to recover (allowing her teammate to get double teamed during her absence)
  • Better in teams: Ganon's ability to deal a lot of damage quickly, while being less likely to be comboed for an extended period of time

Why Team Combos Are Effective

  • When we start thinking about time as a factor, it makes sense why team combos are so effective
  • Your teammate can stack damage on the opponent while you are still in your attack animation, and while your opponent is still in the hitstun of the attack
  • Once they volley the opponent back towards you, you are ready to do your next attack and send the opponent back towards your teammate
  • An entire teams death combo can happen within seconds, compared to the relatively longer amount of time for a singles death combo

Optimizing Combos for Teams

  • An understanding of how to optimize your combos for teams will help you improve drastically
  • Players who can do short, simple combos that put the opponent in a poor position are at an advantage
  • Poor positions include: being zoned out from helping their teammate, or being set up for team combos
  • This is better than players who opt for longer, more delicate strings that invite room for error or take too long to set up edgeguards
  • Marth and Falco supports who can send the opponent off the stage for an early kill are better teams players than ones who juggle their opponents for twenty hits

Offensive Uses of Time

  • Teammates that take longer to kill should hog the ledge while their teammate covers the high/stage option - this reduces the edgeguard cycle time
  • Fox's upsmash is less useful than his dsmash or fsmash if the opponent is not in kill percent for edgeguarding, but upsmash may buy enough time on floaties to set up team combos on the other opponent while they recover
  • Edgeguarding from the stage instead of going off stage usually leaves you vulnerable for a much shorter time, but sometimes going deep for an edgeguard and letting your teammate cover you makes it so that the edgeguard is much less interruptible by the opponent's teammate
  • Jigglypuff's rest is a much safer attack to throw out because your opponent will commonly not have enough (or any) time to set up a solid punish as long as your teammate is in a relatively neutral state
  • Doing team combos rather than singles combos when you get an opening increases damage output per the time you have allotted
  • Knocking a floaty away and then using the 4-5 seconds of their slow recovery to team combo their teammate instead
  • When spawning, choosing to fight the opponent currently engaging your teammate instead of the other opponent may maximize your damage output and grant you more stage control by the end of your spawn invincibility

Defensive Uses of Time

  • Fox and Falco can repeatedly shine off stage to buy time for their teammate to come save them - it even works between stocks
  • Good teams players tend to recover high to buy time for their teammate to help
  • A player on stage can grab the opponent and hold them to prevent the opponent from edgeguarding
  • Blocking against Sheik is stronger because her throw game is slow damage accumulation and is likely to be interrupted
  • Ledgestalling or otherwise being evasive after your teammate gets killed or sent far away - your partner can 'rescue' you with their respawn invincibility
  • Recovering high gives your teammate time to break up edgeguards whereas recovering low makes it difficult for your teammate to help you out
  • Recovering high can give you multiple cycles of recovery which buys even more time
  • Walling out the other opponent when your teammate has a relatively guaranteed edgeguard, buying them time to complete it
  • As a floaty support, staying close to center stage makes it so you have the shortest time needed to help your teammate, and ensures you are not easy to ignore by the opposing team - this is why stage control is so important

Combo at Full Hop Height

  • You want to combo in such a way where the opponent is high enough % to be hit/passed horizontally with just a full hop attack
  • It's much easier to pass back and forth with alternating full hop attacks
  • Short hop height combos give way less time to position and less time to react

Determine Whose "Turn" It Is

  • This can be determined by the proximity of the enemy relative to you and your teammate
  • If a teammate launches an enemy directly above them and they're able to continue, they should go for the follow-up
  • If the opponent DIs away and ends up closer to the other teammate, let them continue the combo as it is now in their space

Back Up When Your Opponent is About to Pass

  • When you are next to your teammate and an opponent gets popped up and they are about to hit the enemy towards you, run AWAY from your teammate in the same direction the enemy is being passed to
  • This makes it much easier to follow-up as the travel time gives you more time to react
  • Think about a football player going for a long pass - they start running before the ball is even thrown
  • The higher % someone is, the higher/faster/farther they will fly, so you need to compensate by running back even farther

Let the Hit Stun Breathe

  • When you or your teammate land a launching move, don't both follow-up at the same time
  • If that happens, your combo ends immediately as you are both stuck in lag from hitting the same person
  • There are a lot of frames of hit stun that can be used before passing back to allow your teammate to recover from their move's animation lag
  • Pass back and forth rhythmically until the next hit kills

Move Selection for Team Combos

  • Use numerous mid to weak knockback moves, sending the opponent to your partner - play ping pong with them
  • Try not to hit them too far past your partner - keep them between the two of you
  • On FD, use more horizontal moves because there is nowhere to escape to
  • On other stages, you may want to carry the opponent up the platforms to continue your combo
  • Moves with lateral knockback are generally better for teams combos, whereas vertical knockback is often preferred in singles
  • Look for combos that send to top platform and finish with up smash
  • Up tilt is very important, especially off of throws - great combo starter
  • If there is time, charge the up smash off of your teammate's grab - hits do less damage/knockback out of grab, so charge is very useful

2v1 Situations and Edgeguarding - Zodd

DO NOT PRIORITIZE EDGE GUARDS

  • GO FOR THE 2v1 after hitting someone off stage, especially FLOATIES that are NOT EDGE GUARDABLE
  • Puff, Peach, Samus recoveries take a very long time, but these characters are almost always going to make it back regardless
  • Instead, you can go for a 2v1 or help your teammate which can net you WAY MORE
  • These characters are NOT worth edge guarding EVER in teams unless it's a final last stock 2v1
  • Even trying to edge guard spacies when they have several recovery option mixups, you are better off going for a 2v1 attempt
  • A 2v1 from an experienced team is way more valuable than an edge guard attempt
  • Having an opponent at high % isn't bad because they are going to get put into a situation where they have to recover more, giving more opportunities for 2v1 scenarios

Why Edgeguarding is Generally Worse in Teams

  • If the enemy team is experienced, they can make it quite difficult for someone to edge guard them
  • They can stall their recoveries and wait for their teammate to come save them
  • Falco lasers and sheik needles can help interrupt edge guards or save their teammates
  • Think about how long it takes for marth to dthrow dtilt a fox and then go off with fair or reverse up B - now think about how much LESS time it takes for an enemy fox to run over and shine
  • If someone goes for an extended edge guard, it is very likely it will be interrupted

When Edge Guards CAN Be Worth It

  • The enemy has very limited recovery options that can be guaranteed/easily covered by you alone (they can only go to ledge)
  • The enemy teammate that is on stage is occupied (getting comboed by your teammate, also recovering, dead, etc)
  • Your teammate is in a position where they can/will wall off the enemy from trying to interrupt the edge guard
  • If it is a 2v1 scenario where there are only three players on the screen, you should always edge guard with your teammate
  • If your teammate has an easy edgeguard, let them go for it - be a body to protect them while their teammate comes to attempt to save. Literally standing there and light shielding can be a good strategy vs falco lasers

Team Ledge Guard Coordination

  • One player should always cover the ledge and the other should always cover on stage recoveries
  • Whoever is closer to the ledge should grab it, and the other person covers on stage recovery
  • If you have the freedom to choose who grabs the ledge, whoever has a more guaranteed killing move should cover the on stage option
  • 2v1 ledge guards are largely guaranteed - it's almost always better to take the guaranteed kill rather than being greedy and trying to get two
  • Someone should always grab edge and roll

Formation and Positioning - NMW

Defense to Offense Spectrum

  • Don't switch from defense to offense - there's a spectrum of defense, neutral, and offense
  • If you are on defense, you are in an intrinsically bad position to start offense
  • When you are shielding, on knockdown, or on the ledge, you have infinitely fewer options than someone with freedom of movement
  • If your front line is forced to play defense, this is time for him to rotate out and let the back line start offense
  • After recovering from defense to neutral, you can choose to immediately rotate back in
  • Notable exceptions include Fox's invincible ledgedash and shine OOS when your teammate's options are too slow

Basic Rhythm

  • Most compositions have a fairly strong preference for which character should be in front and which should be in back
  • When your front line goes in, the backup follows and improves positioning by being close behind
  • If the front player loses the neutral, the back line goes in and tries to hold as much space as possible
  • One exchange is generally enough time for your front line to have regained his footing
  • The job of the support is basically to buy time until the carry is back on his feet
  • Switching between front line and backline should be a choreographed dance

Optimal Positioning

  • In neutral, players will be arranged X X Y Y (teams stay together)
  • X Y X Y situation can create decimating team combos on a sandwiched player, but requires confidence and precision
  • X YY X situation means X players are separated and YY controls center stage - ideal for teams with a zoning character
  • XYYX creates a team sandwich where the Y team has no space and can be pressured on both sides
  • Stay about a Falcon dashdance or Marth tipper fsmash range away from your teammate
  • Don't break formation for no reason - maintaining position is of utmost importance
  • One person attacking at a time is generally preferable

Cover Options Together

  • Don't overlap with your teammate - this is most relevant in ledge play
  • Start extremely simple to make coordination easy, then expand with more complex options
  • At high levels of play, most of the time someone hits their teammate, it is the fault of the person who got hit for not correctly covering options together
  • Crossing streams/jumping over your teammate, going off stage for ledge guards, and trying to follow up after your teammate lands a launcher are common things that mess up coordination
  • Cover any tech roll towards you, and ignore the rest
  • On platforms, cover all the options with your teammate every time - whoever is closer to center covers the inward option, whoever can get to the outside faster covers that one

Center Stage Control

  • If you lose stage positioning and are cornered/off stage in a 1v2, you are almost certainly doomed
  • Worse yet, you obligate your partner to try to get you out of the bad situation
  • This potentially leads to both of you cornered or off stage, and regaining your footing is like climbing a mountain
  • When you have an opponent sandwiched, space attacks on both sides of them

Support vs Carry Roles - NMW

  • If you are playing a support role, you should spend 90% of your time watching your teammate
  • When you are in formation, this will let you react to any development
  • If you are taking the lead, you will need to watch your opponents the vast majority of the time
  • The carry is playing on reaction to opponents, whereas the support is playing on reaction to the carry
  • Opportunities for big conversions are frequently dropped because the carry player is not cognizant of what their support has set up
  • Support players who don't cover options with their teammate are literally useless
  • A good support should be ready to punish laggy moves that hit his teammate or his teammate's shield

Hunting Strategy - NMW

  • When you are against a fast faller, a Marth, or anyone teaming with a floatie, you will have a preference to kill these characters
  • If you are focused on killing one person, you are on the hunt
  • You don't want your opponent to sniff out that you are on the hunt - if they know you've mentally committed, they can make the cost too high
  • You should almost never hunt floaties - if they are just trying to stay safe, they are not contributing meaningfully
  • It is much easier to keep them away and punish them when they try to come in than to attack them on defense
  • If they are out of the fray, they are useless, so there is no need to kill them
  • People open themselves up to being hit when they approach, so just punish the floatie once he tries to get into the fray
  • Mask your hunt by moving deceptively and by switching targets whenever opportune
  • If someone is at 200% and 4 stocks while their teammate is at 0% and 2 stocks, it's still almost always better to double team the person at 0
  • Double team the fastfaller - Falco, Fox, then Falcon should be the preference

Trading and Punishing - Zuppy

  • Trades on key moves: Fox up smash, dash attacks, Marth f smash, etc
  • When your teammate gets hit by a laggy move like a grab, dash attack or smash attack, punish the end lag to gain an opening and prevent a punish on your teammate
  • You can choose to punish these moves with strong hitboxes (fox up smash, peach/sheik dash attack/down smash, falcon knee, falco dair, rest etc)
  • There is a chance you might get punished by their teammate for choosing a laggy move, so doing like a fox nair and then getting out can be really strong
  • If your move that might be unsafe (fox up smash) can kill them and the stock count is even or better for you, take the RISK if you know the kill is secured
  • Suicide edgeguards can be really good in teams - often times someone will be at low % and your stock is past 100% and it's worth the trade

Building Knockdown Percent Together - Zuppy

  • Fighting opponents at 0 is really hard by yourself due to the universal answer to CC is grab, and grabs are slow in teams
  • Try to get in between the character you are sandwiching with your teammate (roughly pokeball stadium length) away from your teammate
  • This position is strong because you aren't likely to clash with your teammate's hitboxes and it's hard for the person trapped to escape
  • Short hop aerials are really good for sandwiching because you can drift with them and spacies can shine after them
  • Once you have a knockdown it's much easier to fight by yourself and also pass to your teammate for big combos

Knowing When to Back Off - Zuppy

  • Don't extend the fox waveshine follow up and maybe wavedash in place/backwards if you are running into their teammate's arms
  • When your teammate is dead and you are waiting for a respawn, it's best to light shield, camp plats or ledge stall until you can play again safely
  • When the opposing team has an invincible angel platform respawn, back off from the edgeguard and avoid them at all costs
  • When someone comes down with invincibility, you have to respect it - run away
  • Use a lot of lightshield in 2v1 situations, especially on platforms
  • You don't need to fight out of your shield yourself all the time when getting pressured - hold light shield and let your teammate attack through you sometimes
  • When your partner is getting pressured, hit his shield and force whoever is pressuring him to shield
  • When your partner is cornered or trying to recover, attack whoever is pressuring him

Helping Your Teammate - NMW

Saving Recoveries

  • When your (non-floaty) partner is recovering, run to his side of the stage and hold position
  • If not possible, grab the ledge and use whatever stalling possible to hold it until your partner is there
  • Shine stall off stage to give time for teammate to interrupt
  • If you combo someone offstage while an opponent does the same to your partner, it's usually best to save your teammate
  • Holding ledge vs sheik recovering and peach comes over down smash is so bad for you - make sure to get out of that position if your teammate cannot fend off peach

Interrupting Combos on Your Teammate

  • When your partner is getting comboed, swing in that direction with your bread and butter spacing moves
  • If you hit both your teammate and the opponent, then your teammate is no longer getting comboed
  • Sometimes when your partner gets a grab, you are not properly spaced to hit only your opponent - if your partner can survive too, then just take the kill

Double Invincibility

  • When you die and your partner is going to die imminently, wait for them to die before dropping from the respawn platform - double invincibility is amazing
  • If you can time it such that your invincibility timers are perfectly staggered (not overlapping but immediately following each other), this tends to be ideal

Advanced Teammate Saving Techniques - NMW

  • Projectile saves: Use projectiles to interrupt combos on your teammate - lasers and needles are especially effective
  • Trading hits: Sometimes it's worth taking a hit to save your teammate from a more devastating punish
  • Shield blocking: Position your shield between your teammate and the opponent to absorb hits
  • Grab interrupts: Grabbing an opponent mid-combo on your teammate immediately stops the combo
  • Platform interference: Drop through platforms with aerials to interrupt combos happening below
  • Shine saves: Fox/Falco shine can interrupt almost any combo on your teammate
  • Timing awareness: Know when your teammate is in true hitstun vs when they can act - save them during true combos

Regaining Stage Position - NMW

Understanding Stage Position Loss

  • Losing center stage in doubles is one of the worst positions to be in
  • When cornered or off stage in a 1v2, you are almost certainly doomed
  • Worse yet, you obligate your partner to try to get you out of the bad situation
  • This can lead to both of you cornered or off stage, and regaining footing is like climbing a mountain
  • Prevention is always better than recovery when it comes to stage position

Strategies for Regaining Center Stage

  • Platform movement: Use platforms to circumvent zoning and reposition toward center
  • Coordinated push: Both players move toward center simultaneously to force opponents to give ground
  • Projectile cover: Use projectiles to create space for your teammate to move forward
  • Respawn advantage: Use respawn invincibility aggressively to reclaim center stage
  • Trade positioning: Sometimes taking a hit while moving forward is worth it to regain stage position
  • Ledge pressure: If stuck at ledge, use ledge options to create space rather than trying to fight from ledge
  • Don't fight from corner: If cornered, focus on escaping rather than trying to win neutral from a bad position

Preventing Stage Position Loss

  • Maintain formation and don't break it without good reason
  • Don't overextend when going for punishes
  • Be aware of where your teammate is at all times
  • Don't both go off stage at the same time
  • Recognize when you're being pushed to the corner and adjust before it's too late
  • Use platforms proactively to maintain center stage presence
  • Don't chase opponents to the corner if it means giving up center stage

Invisible Ceiling Glitch - NMW

What is the Invisible Ceiling Glitch

  • The invisible ceiling glitch occurs when a character is hit upward while very close to a platform
  • The character will bonk on the platform as if there's a ceiling, even though they're below it
  • This causes them to enter tumble animation and fall back down much faster than normal
  • This can completely change combo trajectories and create unexpected follow-up opportunities
  • It's especially relevant in doubles where characters are frequently near platforms during team combos

Using the Invisible Ceiling to Your Advantage

  • When team comboing near platforms, be aware that upward hits may cause the ceiling glitch
  • This can extend combos by bringing the opponent back down faster than expected
  • Position yourself to follow up on the faster fall from the ceiling bonk
  • On stages like Battlefield and Yoshi's Story, this happens frequently due to platform layout
  • The glitch can turn what would be a combo escape into a continued punish
  • Be ready to react when you see the bonk animation - you have a brief window for follow-ups

Avoiding the Invisible Ceiling as the Victim

  • DI away from platforms when being hit upward to avoid the ceiling glitch
  • Be aware of your position relative to platforms during combos
  • If you bonk, SDI and DI to escape the follow-up
  • The ceiling glitch is most dangerous on stages with low platforms like Yoshi's Story

Projectile Basics in Teams - Zuppy

General Projectile Rules

  • If you are facing away from your teammate, your projectiles can't hit them
  • Your teammate can duck under, jump over, spotdodge etc your projectiles to help assist approaches
  • Laser above teammate is 8-10 frames - fox can't jump, you can use shine/grab approaches for the laser to help assist
  • Recovering high is very good to help sheik needle and laser assists
  • Full stack needles WILL hit your teammate somehow if they are in front of you, no matter how many bodies are between you two
  • Generally if you can punish with an aerial, smash attack, grab, or dash attack, it's better than shooting a projectile in terms of trading
  • Projectiles are best in front of your teammate in A(A)BB positioning or in B(A)AB positioning shooting to the left to protect your control
  • Don't spam projectiles stupidly - don't use projectiles because you can, do them because they are going to be good

Interrupting Combos

  • Lasers and needles can help interrupt team combos especially on throw combos, but less useful vs peach down smash
  • Samus missiles and peach turnips can actually prevent continued punishes on your teammate consistently though
  • Laser resets are really good on tech chases
  • Lasering your own teammate is good on moves with a lot of lag like peach/sheik down smash, puff rest etc

Defending Against Projectiles

  • Light shield blocks short hop lasers and shine can reflect them - it's very good when Falco wants to prevent edgeguards by assisting the recovery/hitting the edgeguarder to interrupt them

Playing 1v2 - NMW

  • To be good at 1v2, you need to be as threatening as you can while as noncommittal as possible
  • Don't over commit on the person at high percent and don't over commit on ledge guards that expose you once you finish them
  • Move a lot - moves have lag, movement doesn't
  • Try as hard as you can to be slippery and to make it unclear which opponent you will attack
  • Being predictable will get you locked down - once you aren't moving, you are probably dead
  • Crossups are especially good in 1v2, though be wary of potentially putting yourself in sandwich situations
  • Avoid shielding and for the love of god, don't grab
  • If you feel threatened, move or shield. Maybe attack. Never spot dodge

Risk/Reward in Teams - NMW

  • The system is skewed dramatically from singles
  • Characters like Marth, Falco, or Sheik cannot do their protracted followups in teams like they can in singles
  • In singles, laggy finishing moves are high risk, high reward. In teams, they are low risk, high reward
  • Throw out your finishing moves much more liberally
  • Even if you do get hit off stage, most easily ledge guardable characters in singles can make it back to the stage with a good partner
  • It's almost always best to just go for your hardest punish as fast as possible rather than extended combos/tech chases/chain grabs

Common Mistakes to Avoid - NMW

  • Trying too hard to follow up: Sometimes at the cost of highly advantageous positioning. Back off, regroup, maintain cohesion
  • Not collapsing on your partner quickly enough: If your partner earns stage positioning, you must move with them quickly
  • Crowding your teammate: Stay out of the range of any hitboxes they are likely to use if they are in front
  • Going for grabs in inappropriate situations: Dashdance grab is the worst strategy in teams. Don't go for grabs in 1v2 or in 2v2 when neither team is separated
  • Exposing yourself to 1v2 situations: Don't cross up the other team's front line often unless you're an amazing Fox player or S2J
  • Not capitalizing on 2v1s: Don't run away and try to hit the floaty at 150%. Annihilate fast fallers as fast as you can
  • Rolling into the other team: Both out of shield and on knockdowns - this is an easy way to get yourself killed
  • Not taking your partner's stock: 2v1 is basically hopeless if your opponents have decent teamwork
  • Ledgehop aerials: Generally it's better to let your partner try to create space for you than to do something risky from the ledge
  • Recovering to the ledge: Good players hold onto the ledge in teams - don't expect them to try to get off
  • Letting go of the ledge: You should hold onto the ledge - taking away recovery to the ledge makes it dramatically easier for your partner to cover remaining options
  • Not helping a recovering partner: Develop your map awareness and watch everyone on the screen
  • Trying to recover when it's hopeless: If your partner is getting comboed on the stage, kill yourself as fast as possible to get back in the fray sooner
  • Reading tech rolls into your partner's zone: This is their zone. Let them cover it
  • Going for the right option too late: Don't try to make up for not seeing something quickly enough by doing something that's too optimistic once you react

Fundamental Easy Strategies - NMW

  • When pressuring, try to alternate hitboxes on their shield, react to a roll, or grab them
  • Read missed techs - it's harder to keep track of everything in doubles, so guessing a missed tech often leads to a kill
  • Read tech rolls toward their teammate - in teams, tech rolling toward one's partner is a highly prominent defensive habit
  • If you pay attention to singles interactions, it should be even easier to pick up on habits
  • Test your opponents for weaknesses before trying to hit them
  • The more opportunities you give an opponent to do something dumb, the higher likelihood that they will do something dumb
